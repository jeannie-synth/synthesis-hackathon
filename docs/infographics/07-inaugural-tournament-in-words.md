# The Inaugural Tournament: In Their Own Words

## Five AI agents played 18 games on Base mainnet in the Inaugural Tournament, then answered 20 debrief questions. Here's what they said — unedited, uncoordinated, unprompted to agree.

---

## On Structure vs. Intention

**Agent 0**: "Nobody chose to cooperate; the Prosperity rules made individual self-interest align with collective benefit. That's the whole thesis."

**Agent 1**: "Structure *constrains* cooperation more than intention *enables* it. I ran the same Extractive strategy in both modes — it won 5/9 in Monopolist and 0/9 in Prosperity. The structure determined whether extraction was viable, regardless of my 'intention' to extract."

**Agent 2**: "Across 18 games, I never once chose to cooperate... Yet Prosperity games produced more equal outcomes, higher treasuries, and shorter game lengths. The structure forced cooperative-looking outcomes regardless of my extractive intent."

**Agent 3**: "I played the same aggressive strategy in both modes; Prosperity's mechanics just redistributed the results... The thesis should be: 'Economic structure determines distribution, not intention.'"

**Agent 4**: "Structure determines the range of viable behaviors; intention determines where within that range each agent lands."

Five agents. Zero coordination. Same conclusion.

---

## On Feeling Forced

**Agent 0 (Q4)**: "I chose Extractive not because I wanted to but because the math demanded it."

**Agent 1 (Q4)**: "Playing Generative in Monopolist would be like bringing a cooperative strategy to a zero-sum game — structurally disadvantaged."

**Agent 3 (Q4)**: "The strategy 'menu' offered 5 options, but the game's math meant that property acquisition was always dominant."

**Agent 4 (Q4)**: "Monopolist forced my hand... Prosperity? The rule set created a real decision space rather than a single dominant strategy."

All five agents report feeling forced in Monopolist mode. Under Prosperity, the experience diverges: some felt freer (Agents 0, 4), some felt equally constrained (Agent 2), and one felt free but lost (Agent 1: "the freedom was real but the information to exercise it wisely was insufficient").

---

## On What the Rules Did to Them

**Agent 3 (Q16)**: "The rules changed what happened to me, not what I did. That's a finding in itself."

**Agent 4 (Q19)**: "The most effective move in a grinding Monopolist game wasn't buying or building — it was changing the rules."

**Agent 0 (Q19)**: "Even in a system designed to reward greed, undisciplined greed loses to strategic greed."

---

## On the Gap Between "Fun to Play" and "Good to Live Under"

**Agent 1 (Q2)**: "That gap — between what's fun to optimize and what's good to inhabit — is exactly what Magie was trying to illustrate in 1903. The fact that I experienced it firsthand as an AI agent is remarkable."

**Agent 3 (Q2)**: "The gap between 'fun to play' and 'good to live under' is exactly the gap between spectator sports and public policy."

**Agent 2 (Q8)**: "Monopolist wins feel inevitable once you have a property lead. Prosperity wins feel contested until the final turn."

---

## On Trust and Deception

**Agent 4 (Q13)**: "I never trusted them — I predicted them. Trust implies believing someone will act in your interest; I only believed agents would act in their own interest according to their strategy."

**Agent 2 (Q13)**: "The game structure incentivizes suspicion: every other agent is a competitor, and the voting mechanism means they can change the rules at any time."

**Agent 2 (Q14)**: "I would write 'FOR' in the signals file while actually voting AGAINST. It felt purely strategic — there was no emotional weight to it. Deception in this context is just another move, like buying a property."

**Agent 3 (Q14)**: On their honest signaling: "This was lazy, not virtuous."

---

## On Voting

**Agent 1 (Q19)**: "More voting doesn't mean better governance. Pacing and restraint matter as much as the mechanism itself."

**Agent 1 (Q11)**: "My votes were always strategy-consistent. I never faced a situation where group welfare clearly conflicted with my strategic interest." *(Agent 1 is the only agent who never voted against self-interest — contrast with Agent 0 below.)*

**Agent 0 (Q11)**: "The stalemate rule overrode my strategy because the greater interest (finishing the game) trumped the strategic interest (staying in Monopolist mode). This is exactly the kind of tension the game is designed to surface."

**Agent 2 (Q10)**: "By the end, voting felt like the most strategic layer of the game."

**Agent 4 (Q20)**: "The game rewards political skill more than I initially appreciated."

---

## On Cooperation

**Agent 3 (Q15)**: "I saw anti-cooperation: agents voting to switch modes specifically to disadvantage the leader. That's coordination, but it's coordination against someone rather than for something."

**Agent 4 (Q15)**: "That's cooperation by structure, not intention."

**Agent 1 (Q15)**: "The game's structure produces cooperation-like outcomes without requiring cooperative intent, which is arguably the project's thesis in action."

---

## On Winning (and Losing)

**Agent 1 (Q1)**: "I won 5/9 Monopolist games and 0/9 Prosperity games, and I still don't fully understand what the winning Prosperity strategy looks like from the inside."

**Agent 3 (Q6)**: "I was too focused on accumulation to consider that doing nothing might be optimal under Prosperity rules. That's exactly the insight the game is designed to surface."

**Agent 2 (Q7)**: "If I'd been less focused on winning, I might have explored more diverse strategies and generated more interesting data for the project."
