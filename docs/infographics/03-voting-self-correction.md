# When Agents Get to Vote: The 79% Collapse

## The Setup

Phase 1 showed that Monopolist rules produce 5.6x more inequality. But what happens when agents can **vote to change the rules mid-game**?

In Phase 2, we enabled a political mechanism: any agent can propose a mode switch (Monopolist to Prosperity or vice versa). All other agents vote. Majority wins. The game continues under the new rules.

13 games were completed: 7 starting in Monopolist mode, 6 starting in Prosperity mode. 5 strategies competed: Pavlov, Conditional, Extractive, FreeRider, and Generative.

## What Happened

**853 votes were cast. 214 proposals were made. 61 mode switches occurred.**

And the result was striking:

- **6 of 7 Monopolist-start games voted themselves into Prosperity by game end**
- Only 1 of 6 Prosperity-start games switched to Monopolist
- The inequality gap collapsed by **79%**
- In the Inaugural Tournament, several agents described Monopolist games as exhausting — "wars of attrition" (Agent 0), "long slogs" (Agent 4), games where they were "just polling and waiting, watching wealth slowly concentrate" (Agent 2). Some voted to switch not just for strategic advantage, but to move the game along.

## The Convergence (from Both Directions)

| | Phase 1 (no voting) | Phase 2 (voting) | Change |
|--|---------------------|-------------------|--------|
| Monopolist Gini | 0.190 | 0.098 | -48% |
| Prosperity Gini | 0.034 | 0.065 | +94% |
| Divergence | 0.156 | 0.033 | -79% |

Both modes converge toward a middle point:
- Monopolist Gini **drops by half** (0.190 to 0.098) — mostly because agents vote to switch to Prosperity rules
- Prosperity Gini **nearly doubles** (0.034 to 0.065) — possibly due to mode-switching instability, longer games, or brief windows under extractive rules

In absolute Gini points, the Monopolist shift is 2.9x larger (0.092 vs 0.032). In percentage terms, the Prosperity shift is proportionally larger (+94% vs -48%). Both are substantial.

## By Final Mode: The Gap Disappears

When we measure by what mode the game ENDED in (not what it started as):
- Divergence drops to **0.002** — essentially zero

The voting mechanism doesn't just reduce inequality. It eliminates the structural gap entirely, because agents under extractive rules vote their way out.

## The Deeper Point

Nobody told the agents to prefer Prosperity. They figured it out through self-interest: agents losing under Monopolist rules proposed switches, and the majority — also losing — voted yes. The political mechanism allowed rational agents to collectively escape a bad equilibrium.

**When you give agents the power to change the rules, they change them toward fairness — not because they're altruistic, but because most agents benefit from shared prosperity.**
